Background

The Starting Point

MediaTek's Global Volunteer Hero Recognition (GVH) is an annual CSR program celebrating employees who dedicate their time to community volunteering. In 2025, the program ran largely on emails, Google Forms, and manual spreadsheets.

The program had three pain points: employees didn't know it existed, those who did couldn't figure out how to apply, and judges couldn't score efficiently across 40+ applications. All three problems were mine to solve — with no developer and no budget.

📢
Problem 1 — Awareness

No dedicated communication channel. Employees learned about GVH through generic newsletters buried in inboxes.

✍️
Problem 2 — Application Friction

"I don't know how to write my story" was the #1 reason employees didn't apply, despite having meaningful volunteer work.

📊
Problem 3 — Judging Chaos

Judges scored via email and Excel with no rubric consistency, no progress tracking, and no central view of results.

💡
My Approach

Build three targeted products — each solving one problem — using AI-assisted vibe coding. No engineering team. Ship in weeks.

What I Learned

The PM Decisions That Mattered

The hardest part wasn't the code — it was the product thinking behind each design choice.

  • 01 Treat Jarvis as an interview, not a form. People freeze at blank boxes but answer questions naturally. Designing it as a journalist-style dialogue removed the psychological barrier to starting.
  • 02 Build the review rubric into the scoring UI, not a separate PDF. Previous cycles had inconsistent scores because judges had to reference external guidelines. Embedding 4-tier descriptors next to each slider eliminated the gap.
  • 03 Design for two network environments from day one. Knowing judges would mix corporate wifi (firewall) and personal networks, I built two parallel data paths — auto-sync and manual CSV — with clear UX prompts for each scenario.
  • 04 The campaign site's job is to remove anxiety, not showcase features. The FAQ isn't a legal document — it's a reassuring conversation with a skeptical employee. Tone is a product decision.
  • 05 Three products, one coherent user journey. Each product hands off to the next: Site → Jarvis → Platform. The ecosystem works because the CTA architecture connects them, not because each piece is individually great.
"The best tool I had wasn't a framework — it was knowing exactly what problem each product needed to solve."

This project started as a communications task. It became a full product lifecycle: problem definition, user research (asking why people didn't apply), system design, build, and iteration. Doing it with no dev background forced me to be rigorous about requirements — because vague prompts produce vague products. Every feature earned its place by solving a real friction point.
